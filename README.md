<h2>Under the Blazing Sun: Robin's fight for Survival and Dreams</h2>
<div class="meta">By Sojourner News</div>

<img class="article-img" src="FEATURED WRITING.jpeg" alt="Robin Story Image">

<p>
Robin, a street vendor in stands beside his source of livelihood; a pushcart that sells pineapple juice along the streets of Malolos, Bulacan.

Some dreams are meant to be achieved for most people, but for Robin John Dizon, a 20 year old construction worker, dreams are like beautiful stars and forever out of reach.

He started working at the age of 16 years old at Ilo-ilo to support his studies. He graduated junior high school, despite of determination, financial struggles forced him to let go his academic path and set aside his dream of becoming a soldier.

His parents, burdened by financial difficulties, were no longer able to support his schooling and personal needs. Their situation made Robin to make a difficult decision at a young age, he left his hometown Ilo-ilo and moved to Bulacan to search for better opportunities. For him, he realized that survival comes first, even if it meant leaving his comfort zone and unfinished dreams.

When asked about his decision to leave ilo-ilo, Robin shared that it is because of the lack of opportunities and stable work in his province. Although unfamiliar and uncertain with his new environment, he chose to take risk in hopes of sustaining his life through hard work.

Since he stepped in Bulacan, he spends his days working under the sun in construction sites. He tried working as a packer in Lazada and Shopee, but he quit after a month and went back to being a construction worker.

Heat is one of  his relentless opponent in his job. Working under the blazing sun made his work three times harder. Yet despite those hardships, Robin continues to push forward, driven by necessity and determination.

For the meantime, Robin is residing at his aunt's friend, Ate Bibi, because even though he is now working, Robin still struggles to fully support himself and live on his own. Robin's life remains difficult and independence is a goal that he is working hard to to achieve.

In his free time, instead of choosing to rest, he joins Ate bibi in selling pineapple juice in Malolos, Bulacan to help her and as a way of showing his gratitude for letting him stay at her place.

Robin's daily struggle is a story of resilience, hardships, sacrifices and unspoken dream of a young man who continues to work under the sun, hoping that one day, his life will no longer feel like in  surviving mode.
</p>

<h2>Resilience shall never be the key</h2>
<div class="meta">Sojourner Editorial</div>

<img class="article-img" src="OPINION WRITING.jpeg" alt="Flood Protest Image">

<p>
Residents of Hagonoy, Bulacan wade through flood waters as 
“Tindig Hagonoy” protests occurs.

When problem calls, resilience is not the answer 

Resilience is what people hold on to when things get tough. It keeps them to endure, to survive. Yet, in places in the province of Bulacan, resilience begins to feel less like strength and more like a nightmare dressed as concealed strength.

In some parts of Bulacan, people no longer ask when the flood will come. They simply ask how long they will have to live with it. The water does not arrive as a visitor anymore; it seems like a permanent tenant inside their homes.

Residents in multiple barangays in Hagonoy have endured chest-deep floods for weeks, walking kilometers through muddy water just to reach work, school, or even basic services.

In an exclusive interview with Nanay Lucia, a resident of Barangay San Sebastian for over two decades, the change is painfully clear.

“Bumabaha noon pero hindi ganito kalaki,” Nanay Lucia said. “Parang sanap lang siya. Yung maliit lang siya. Hindi kagaya ngayon na sobrang laki. Malaki kasi tinaas ng kalsada.”

Her words reveal something often overlooked and hidden behind in the shadows of inquiries, a lot of development often forgets to include those who should benefit it—its constituents. Roads are elevated, but homes are left behind—literally lower, more vulnerable, and more exposed to flooding. Infrastructure rises, but so does the water.

And then comes the deeper irony.

The controversial “ghost” flood control projects flagged in reports and investigations—have placed Bulacan at the center of scrutiny. Billions of pesos have been allocated for what they claim to be the "solution" for the ongoing flooding yet the lived reality tells a different story. If anything, the floods seem to be getting worse.

This is no longer just about natural disasters. It is about systemic failure— all the unfinished projects and infrastructure, ultimately the empty promises that never fully brought into life.

“Kapag nilapit pa namin kasi namin ito sa gobyerno, wala, pangangakuan ka lang,” Nanay Lucia lamented.
“Ano magagawa namin? Hanggang sa oo lang kami, oo lang din sila.”

It rungs a hollow and a terrifying reality. There is no sense of anger, not even surprise—just numbness and acceptance.

And perhaps that is the most alarming part. When people begin to expect nothing, no pointing fingers if who should be accountable.

And it is blatanly exercised that resilience is repeatedly romanticized. Stories of people continuing life despite hardship, are often framed as inspiring. But resilience, in this context, is not empowering—it is survival in even without the manifestation of real solutions.

Because no community should have to adapt endlessly to a problem that was meant to be solved by the government.

Flooding in Bulacan is often blamed on geography—low-lying land, rivers, high tides. These are real factors. But geography did not mismanage funds. Geography did not normalize inaction.

Governance did.

And so, the floodwaters remain—not just as water, but as a reflection of the uneding neglect.

This is what resilience looks like for many affected citizens, it is never a choice, but an obligation forced upon them. It leaves them to live through it, to adjust, and just endure.

But resilience should not mean settling for less. It should not mean staying silent while the same waters rise, year after year.

Because at some point, the real question is no longer how long people can endure—but how long they should have to.
</p>

</section>

<h2>Heat Index in Bulacan hits Danger Zone; Jeepney Driver endure long trips for family</h2>
<div class="meta">Writer: Micaela Cruz</div>

<img class="article-img" src="BREAKING NEWS1.jpeg" alt="Jeepney Driver Heat">

<p>
MALOLOS BULACAN–The heat index in Bulacan has officially reached the "Danger Zone" level on Saturday, April 18, 2026.

Despite this, jeepney drivers continue to work hard amidst the intense heat that is being felt today. Even though the temperature is extremely high and they bring little amount to their families, they choose to endure it just to provide for their family.

According to PAGASA, the heat index in Bulacan has hit 35°Celcius and has dropped to the "Danger Zone" level. This level poses serious health risks such as heat stroke, exhaustion, heat cramps and other health related issues

One of the most vulnerable sectors are the jeepney drivers...Para kay Francisco Domingo "Kailangan Magtiyaga, Para sa Pamilya,” the heat weather and the little income brought home are not obstacles as long as it is for the family.

Traveling from Meycauayan to Malolos, Francisco  endured a long journey that took one and a half hours, sometimes two hours just to complete a single trip.

Truly, the sacrifices of our drivers are evident. They beat the heat and overcome fatigue because for them, providing for their family is more important than the discomfort they feel.
</p>
